# National Logistics Policy RSM 2024 Source

## Source Identity

This source note extracts the provided file `National Logistics Policy.txt`.

The file appears to be an RSM India January 2024 report titled `National Logistics Policy - Redefining the Future of Indian Logistics`, not the raw Government of India policy document. It summarizes the National Logistics Policy, ULIP, and a national logistics infrastructure navigator covering ports, ICDs, CFSs, MMLPs, railway terminals, dedicated freight corridors, inland waterways, and commercial EVs.

## National Logistics Policy Context

The source frames the National Logistics Policy as a strategic framework unveiled in September 2022 to reduce logistics cost, benchmark services, standardize physical assets/processes/taxonomy, and integrate logistics systems through technology.

Key national context from the source:

- Logistics cost in India was cited around 13-14% of GDP.
- Logistics employed about 2.2 crore people as of 2022.
- India moved about 4.6 billion tonnes of goods annually in the recent period covered by the report.
- Road transport dominates, while rail, coastal shipping, inland waterways, pipelines, and air handle the rest.
- Government initiatives include dedicated freight corridors, coastal and inland shipping capacity, Bharatmala, and road infrastructure development.

## NLP Targets

The source lists three major targets:

- Reduce logistics cost in India to be comparable with global benchmarks by 2030.
- Endeavour to place India among the top 25 countries in the Logistics Performance Index by 2030.
- Create a data-driven decision support mechanism for an efficient logistics ecosystem.

## Strategic Pillars Extracted

The source repeatedly emphasizes:

- Infrastructure development for goods mobility

## MMLP Logic

The source frames MMLPs as temporary storage and transfer centres that support seamless transitions between highways, waterways, railways, and other modes.

Product implication:

- Model MMLPs as physical and information handoff nodes.
- Track dwell, handling, capacity, first-mile, last-mile, document readiness, and mode-chain reliability.
- Use MMLPs in route planning only where transshipment cost and handoff risk are justified by lower total cost or higher reliability.

## EV And eMaaS Logic

The source identifies EV adoption challenges:

- High upfront cost
- Need for charging, financing, servicing, and operational support

It also references eMaaS models:

- Vehicle-as-a-Service
- Battery-as-a-Service

Product implication:

- EV route assignment should use TCO, payload, charging, range, duty cycle, and dwell time.
- EV is strongest first for predictable urban and regional commercial routes.

## Conclusion Extract

The source conclusion argues that India is moving from a consumer-oriented economy toward a production-oriented economy, supported by highways, commercial EVs, multimodal options, inland waterways, sea routes, railway networks, ICDs, and CFSs.

The operational takeaway is that companies must continuously monitor the changing infrastructure landscape and adjust logistics strategy over a 3-5 year horizon.
